The Business Case

A practice branching scenario  ·  Week 11 — Business case development and financial projections

You are a Product Innovation Manager at Optimum Pet Care. Your concept has cleared the gate review and is now in the development phase. The CFO has reviewed your business case and has raised two questions that require a direct response before the development budget is formally released.

This practice scenario puts you in the middle of a business case review — exactly the kind of commercial pressure Assessment 3 will simulate. There is no timer and no submission requirement. Use it to build your confidence with the format and with the kind of financial and commercial reasoning this assessment rewards.

What to notice: A business case is not just a financial model. It is an argument. The CFO's questions in this scenario are designed to test whether your commercial logic is internally consistent — not whether you can produce precise numbers. Pay attention to the difference between what the numbers can and cannot tell you.
Practice Decision Point 1 of 2  ·  Financial Projections Challenge

The Year 1 Projection Is Being Questioned

Week 11 context — Business case development and financial projections

The CFO has reviewed the financial projections in your business case. She accepts the cost structure and the Year 2 and Year 3 growth assumptions. But she has a specific concern about Year 1.

The CFO's Question
"Your Year 1 subscriber acquisition target is 2,400 subscribers at an average monthly spend of $42. That gives you a Year 1 revenue of approximately $1.2 million. My question is simple: how confident are you in that 2,400 subscriber target? Because your acquisition cost assumptions — $18 per subscriber through digital channels — would need to hold precisely for the unit economics to work. If your acquisition cost is actually $25, which is more consistent with what I am seeing in comparable categories, you lose $43,000 on Year 1 subscriber acquisition alone before you factor in fulfilment. How did you arrive at $18?"

Think carefully before choosing. The CFO is not asking you to change the number. She is asking you to defend how you arrived at it. A business case projection that cannot be explained is not a business case — it is a guess. What source or reasoning underlies the $18 acquisition cost assumption in your case?

Consequence  ·  Practice Decision Point 1

Defending the Assumption

What happens next

The CFO accepts the comparable category argument conditionally. She adds a condition to the budget release: if the actual acquisition cost in the first 90 days of launch exceeds $22, the development team must return to the CFO before committing further acquisition spend. The development budget is released with this condition attached. Your projection stands — but so does the risk. The second decision point arrives when the 90-day acquisition data comes in.

Choice recorded: Option A — Defend the $18 assumption.

Consequence  ·  Practice Decision Point 1

A Revised Case

What happens next

You revise the projections to $25 acquisition cost. The Year 1 unit economics are now break-even rather than marginally profitable. The CFO appreciates the honesty. The board approves the development budget with the revised numbers, noting that Year 2 and Year 3 growth needs to be strong to justify the Year 1 investment. You will face a question in the second decision point about how to manage the board's expectations against the new numbers.

Choice recorded: Option B — Revise to $25 acquisition cost.

Consequence  ·  Practice Decision Point 1

Evidence Before Commitment

What happens next

The CFO endorses the pilot proposal. The $5,000 pilot budget is approved immediately. The full development budget is held pending pilot results — expected in six weeks. The pilot runs and returns an actual acquisition cost of $21.50. This is between your original estimate and the CFO's benchmark. You now need to decide how to use this data in the revised business case. That becomes the second decision point.

Choice recorded: Option C — Propose acquisition pilot.

Practice Decision Point 2 of 2  ·  Investor Communication Challenge

The Board Wants a Three-Year Projection

Week 11 context — Business case development and managing financial uncertainty

The board has approved the development budget in principle. Before the final sign-off, the chair has asked for a three-year revenue projection to be presented at the next board meeting. The CFO has asked you to prepare it.

The Challenge
Three-year projections for a new product in a new category are inherently uncertain. The inputs — subscriber growth rate, churn rate, average revenue per user, and customer lifetime value — cannot all be known with confidence. But the board expects a number. A projection that is too conservative may not justify the investment. A projection that is too optimistic sets expectations that the product may not meet and damages credibility when it misses.

You have two viable approaches to presenting the three-year projection, and a third that avoids the problem but introduces a different one.

Think carefully before choosing. Financial projections in a business case are not forecasts — they are arguments. What you are really being asked to show is not what will happen but whether the investment logic is sound under a range of reasonable assumptions. A board that understands this will respond better to honest range-based thinking than to false precision.
Your decision
How do you present the three-year revenue projection?
Consequence  ·  Practice Decision Point 2

A Single Number

What happens next

The board approves the investment. Twelve months later, the actual Year 1 results are 14% below the projected figure. The board chair asks why the projection did not account for this downside. Your footnotes — which did flag this as a risk — are noted but do not fully absorb the credibility damage. A projection with a single line creates a single point of failure. When the actual result misses, the question is always "why did you project that number?"

Consequence  ·  Practice Decision Point 2

Scenario Thinking

What happens next

The board approves the investment. The three-scenario presentation is noted positively by two board members who comment that it reflects mature financial thinking. Twelve months later, the actual result falls between the base case and downside — within the range you presented. The board is able to evaluate the result against a framework you established rather than a single number you committed to. Credibility is maintained.

Consequence  ·  Practice Decision Point 2

Principled Refusal

What happens next

The board chair is initially frustrated. Two other board members find the reasoning sound. After discussion, the twelve-month rolling approach is accepted with modifications — the board requests that a Year 2 projection be prepared at the six-month review rather than at twelve months. Your approach preserved intellectual honesty but created a governance process that required ongoing management. Whether this was the right trade-off depends on your board's appetite for transparency versus certainty.

What This Scenario Practised

Three things to reflect on
  1. What is the difference between a financial assumption and a financial fact? In Assessment 3, your business case section requires three evidence-based claims. The quality of those claims depends on your ability to distinguish between what your evidence can support and what requires an explicit assumption. This is exactly the skill the CFO's question in this scenario was testing.
  2. How does the format force you to commit? In Assessment 3, you cannot present a three-scenario business case — you must make a recommendation. Notice how different that is from this practice scenario's Decision Point 2, where you could choose the scenario approach. Assessment 3 requires you to commit to a position and defend it, even under commercial uncertainty.
  3. What would your Assessment 2 group's evidence base actually support? The most important preparation you can do before Assessment 3 opens is to re-read your Assessment 2 portfolio and identify exactly what claims your group's evidence can and cannot support. The gap between those two things is where your justification will be most valuable — and most revealing.
