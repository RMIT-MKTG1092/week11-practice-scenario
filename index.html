The Investment Committee

A practice branching scenario  ·  Three decisions  ·  Ninety seconds each

You are a Product Innovation Manager presenting to an Investment Committee that will decide whether to fund your concept. Across three decision points you will defend the three claims of your business case under pressure — your target segment claim, your scale claim, and your key risk claim. Each decision arrives with a senior stakeholder pushing back on your reasoning. None of the options is obviously right. Every option costs something concrete.

How this scenario works — read this before you begin

  1. You will identify yourself. First name, last name, and student number are required. The Assessment 3 scenario will ask for the same information. We are practising the discipline of always identifying your work.
  2. You will paste your three claims. Before the scenario begins, you must paste in the three claims you drafted in Tutorial 11.3 — segment, scale, and key risk. The scenario uses your own claims as the source material. You cannot proceed without all three.
  3. Three decision points, ninety seconds each. A countdown timer runs at the top of the screen. When time runs out, the committee makes the decision for you — and you see what that costs. The assessed scenario uses the same timer.
  4. Every choice has consequences. There is no "correct" path. Each option exposes a different cost. The hardest decisions are the ones where you can defend any of the three options.
  5. You will export your record. At the end of the scenario, you will download a Word document with your identification, your pasted claims, your three decisions, and the consequences you triggered. The exported document tells you exactly what to do with it.
Important — the assessment is different from this practice

This practice scenario tests only the business case material from Week 11 — the translation move and the three claims. The Assessment 3 scenario releases at six o’clock on Friday of Week 12 and is different in two ways.

It draws on your full Assessment 1 and Assessment 2 portfolio — not only your three claims. Your Product Innovation Charter, your developed concept, your four lab arguments, your evidence base. A student who only prepares Week 11 materials for Assessment 3 will be underprepared.

You must include your source documents as appendices in your final Assessment 3 submission. The pasted claims, the decision record, your PIC, your lab portfolio entries — all of these become appendices. They demonstrate the traceability of your business case from evidence to recommendation. The exported document at the end of this scenario explains the appendix requirement in detail.

Before you begin, have Tutorial 11.3 open. You will paste your three drafted claims into the scenario in a moment. If you have not drafted them yet, complete the tutorial first — the scenario will not run without them.

Decision 1 of 3  ·  The Segment Claim

The Head of Buying Has Read Your Segment Claim

Investment Committee meeting — 14:32  ·  Boardroom B

The Head of Buying from one of the two largest national grocery retailers sits on your Investment Committee as an external observer. He has been reading your business case during the first ten minutes of the meeting. He looks up from the page where your segment claim is printed in full.

Head of Buying — verbatim

"I have read your segment claim. It is sharp. I respect the discipline. But by my reading of your own evidence, the household type you have defined accounts for under one and a half million homes nationally. We do not list new products for under two million addressable households on national shelves — the slot economics do not work for us, and they will not work for you either. So you have three places to break this. You can broaden the segment, which dilutes the tension your premium pricing depends on. You can stay narrow and accept a specialty-channel-first launch, which removes the volume that recovers your tooling investment inside the original payback horizon. Or you can stay narrow on the segment, accept national distribution at a more modest shelf position, and lengthen your payback horizon to twenty-eight or thirty months instead of twenty-four. Each costs you something different. Take ninety seconds. The committee needs to see how you reason through this — not just what you choose."

Your decision — 90 seconds
Three options. None is wrong. Each one costs in two dimensions at once. Which costs do you accept?
Consequence  ·  Decision 1

A Wider Segment, A Softer Claim

What happens next

The Head of Buying nods. The chair appears satisfied. The Marketing Director, who has been quiet so far, leans forward: "Just to be clear. The original segment was the buyer experiencing a specific tension. The additional households you have just added do not have that tension. So tell me what the wider segment is paying a premium to resolve — because the premium pricing in your case was anchored to that original tension."

You have bought scale at the cost of segment specificity. The premium that supports your scale claim is now decoupled from the tension that justified it. Decision 2 will press on this gap.

What this choice reveals about your reasoning: you protected commercial momentum when the segment claim was challenged. Notice this if it recurs in Decisions 2 and 3.
Consequence  ·  Decision 1

A Specialty-First Defence

What happens next

The Head of Buying acknowledges the choice. The CFO writes something down. The chair speaks: "So you are telling us the segment claim is right and the original channel plan was wrong. That is a meaningful concession to make in this room. Walk us through the specialty-first economics — because if the unit economics do not work without the grocery channel, you have just removed your scale claim by accident."

You have protected one claim by exposing another. The committee will now press the scale claim harder than they would have if you had not made this trade. This is the cost of analytical honesty inside a room that wants a clean story.

What this choice reveals about your reasoning: you protected analytical integrity when commercial momentum was challenged. Notice this if it recurs in Decisions 2 and 3.
Consequence  ·  Decision 1

A Longer Horizon

What happens next

The Head of Buying accepts the secondary-aisle position. The CFO is not yet satisfied: "You have just added six months to the payback horizon. That changes the discount rate the board uses to evaluate this investment. The internal rate of return falls by approximately one and a half percentage points. You have moved this case from comfortably above our hurdle rate to within two points of it." The committee will accept the longer horizon — conditionally — but the scale claim now carries the entire margin of safety.

What this choice reveals about your reasoning: you preserved both claims by accepting a timing cost. This is often the most defensible position but it concentrates risk into the timeline. Notice if you keep choosing structural over time-based trade-offs.
Time elapsed  ·  Decision 1

The Committee Filled the Silence

What happens next

Ninety seconds passed. You did not commit. The Head of Buying glanced at the chair. The chair filled the silence: "Let us return to this question after the next agenda item." It is the most polite way of saying that your segment claim is now registered as a question you could not defend in the room. The committee will return to it. But with less weight on your answer than if you had given one now.

In Assessment 3, a timeout is not a neutral outcome. The scenario records that you did not commit, and the next decision arrives with the previous question still unresolved.

What this reveals about your reasoning: under time pressure you preferred no commitment to a costly one. This is a defensible instinct in some commercial contexts. In an investment committee, it usually is not.

Decision 2 of 3  ·  The Scale Claim

The CFO Has Two Pieces of Analysis, Not One

Investment Committee meeting — 14:48  ·  Boardroom B

The CFO has been listening. She has a Euromonitor printout in front of her. She also has a single-page competitor intelligence memo that arrived from your strategy function yesterday. She slides both across the table.

CFO — verbatim

"I have two pieces of analysis on your scale claim. Both are uncomfortable. First — standard objection — your share assumption against this category implicitly requires taking close to one per cent of share from the incumbents Unilever and Procter and Gamble inside the first eighteen months. The closest comparable launch in the last five years took zero point three per cent in the same window. Second — and this is what is new in this room — we have a credible read that a direct competitor is reaching market six to eight months after your launch with a similar concept. So you are arguing for share that the evidence says is hard to capture, in a market window narrower than your business case assumes. Where does the number come from, and why does it survive the competitive entry assumption?"

What the CFO is testing. Read your scale claim above. The pushback has two heads — the structural one (incumbents will not give up share at the rate you have assumed) and the timing one (the window in which you have the segment to yourself is shorter than your case assumes). Any answer that addresses only one head leaves the other one open.
Your decision — 90 seconds
Three viable answers. The differences are about which costs you accept and which you defer.
Consequence  ·  Decision 2

Differentiation Versus Evidence

What happens next

The CFO reserves judgement. The chair speaks: "We will accept the share claim conditionally, contingent on first-twelve-month consumer trial data confirming the differentiation. If the data does not show a structural difference, we revisit before Year 2 budget is committed." Your scale claim survives but the burden of proof has moved forward. The competitor entry timing is now a parallel threat you cannot mitigate from this room.

What this reveals about your reasoning: you preferred a future-evidence promise to a present-evidence concession. This is a confident move. It compounds if the third decision also pushes the burden forward.
Consequence  ·  Decision 2

A Smaller Number, A Tighter Window

What happens next

The CFO appreciates the concession. The chair is uneasy: "The revised share number does not recover the tooling investment inside the horizon you originally promised. So you have just replaced an aspirational case with a non-viable case unless something else changes. If the accelerated launch costs more than thirty per cent above the original launch budget, we will need to revisit the entire investment thesis." You have protected analytical honesty at the cost of the original investment proposition. The case is now wobbling.

What this reveals about your reasoning: you protected credibility over ambition. This is admirable but expensive in an investment context where the case has to clear a hurdle rate.
Consequence  ·  Decision 2

A Sharper Denominator

What happens next

The CFO leans back. "That is a sharper claim than what you started with. But you have just made the subsegment carry the entire investment case, and the published data on subsegment-level willingness to pay is thinner than the category-level data. I am not arguing the subsegment is wrong — I am arguing the confidence interval around it is wider than your case shows. We will accept the reframe, but we want sensitivity analysis at the 90-day gate showing what happens if the subsegment is 30 per cent smaller than estimated."

What this reveals about your reasoning: you preserved the share number by accepting analytical scrutiny in 90 days. This is rigorous but creates a hard stop you cannot defer.
Time elapsed  ·  Decision 2

A Number Without a Defence

What happens next

The 90 seconds elapsed in silence. The CFO leaves the Euromonitor printout on the table and turns to the chair: "We can come back to it." The committee moves on. Your scale claim is now in the room as an unanswered question. Decision 3 arrives with two open questions sitting behind it. This is what compounding under-preparation looks like.

What this reveals about your reasoning: two timeouts in a row indicate the under-pressure default is to wait rather than commit. Useful information to bring into Assessment 3.

The General Counsel Has Joined Late and the CEO Is on the Line

The General Counsel was not on the original agenda. The chair invited her in fifteen minutes ago after the segment and scale discussion ran long. She has read your risk register on the way down. The CEO has joined via dial-in for this section. She wants a clear recommendation before close of business. The General Counsel speaks before she sits.

General Counsel — verbatim

"I will be direct. Your risk register names a specific risk to the return on this investment. By naming it in the register, you have put this committee on formal notice. What do you propose — today — that resolves the conflict between your risk claim and your investment thesis? Whatever you say, I will be minuting. Take ninety seconds."

Consequence  ·  Decision 3

Defensible But Second

What happens next

The General Counsel co-signs. The CEO accepts the recommendation but instructs the team to model the financial cost of the delay before the next board meeting. Launch is delayed by approximately two months. The competitor reaches market first and takes approximately 22 per cent of the early adopter segment. They are forced to soften their own claims in month three when their evidence is challenged. You arrive second with a defensible position. Whether this was the right trade depends on how recoverable second-mover share is in your category — and the data on that is not settled.

What this reveals about your reasoning: you protected integrity over speed when the stakes were highest. This is the most defensible choice on the rubric. It is rarely the most popular choice in the room.
Consequence  ·  Decision 3

A Softer Claim, A Cleaner Launch

What happens next

The General Counsel co-signs. The Marketing Director objects briefly but loses. The CFO re-runs the case at the lower premium and confirms the payback extends to twenty-eight months. The CEO accepts. Launch proceeds on schedule. The competitor reaches market two weeks after you with a stronger claim — you spend the first six months in a competitive defensibility argument that you would not have had to fight at the original claim level. The decision was honest. The cost of the honesty is now visible.

What this reveals about your reasoning: you protected integrity and timing by accepting a financial cost. This is the most balanced of the three options and the one most likely to receive committee consensus.
Consequence  ·  Decision 3

A Window of Exposure

What happens next

The General Counsel asks for her objection to be minuted. The CEO overrides her. Launch proceeds with the original claim. Three weeks after launch, an industry blog runs an article questioning the claim. Four weeks after launch, the regulator opens an informal inquiry. The brand pulls the lead claim voluntarily in week six and replaces it with a softened version — eight weeks earlier than the original mitigation plan would have required. The premium has now eroded faster than Option B would have produced, and the brand reputation has absorbed the cost of the inquiry. This is the most expensive of the three paths.

What this reveals about your reasoning: you preferred momentum to disclosure when the disclosure had already been made in writing. This is a difficult position to defend after the fact. In an investment context, the cost is reputational as well as financial.
Time elapsed  ·  Decision 3

The Committee Decided Without You

What happens next

Ninety seconds elapsed. You had no answer. The General Counsel turned to the chair: "Without a clear position from the proposer, I cannot co-sign the risk register as it stands. I recommend the committee defer." The chair defers. Two weeks later you bring a return paper. The decision is taken in a smaller room, by people who were not in the original meeting, with less context. The outcome is reasonable. The cost is that the decision was no longer yours to shape.

What this reveals about your reasoning: the third timeout indicates a deep aversion to commitment under uncertainty. This is the most diagnostic of all the timeouts — the pattern is consistent across all three claims.
