The Investment Committee

A practice branching scenario  ·  Three decisions  ·  Ninety seconds each

You are a Product Innovation Manager presenting to an Investment Committee that will decide whether to fund your concept. Across three decision points you will defend the three claims of your business case under pressure — your target segment claim, your scale claim, and your key risk claim. Each decision arrives with a senior stakeholder pushing back on your reasoning. None of the options is obviously right.

In the real Assessment 3 scenario, the decisions will be tailored to your group’s concept and your segment. The practice scenario uses a generic concept so every student can run it. The disciplines being practised are the same.

How this scenario works

  1. Three decision points. Each one tests one of the three business case claims you learned about this week. The challenges are deliberately demanding — this is what investment-committee pressure feels like.
  2. Ninety seconds per decision. A countdown timer runs at the top of the screen. When time runs out, the committee makes the decision for you — and you see what that costs. This mirrors the assessed scenario exactly.
  3. Read the brief, then choose. The fastest readers do not necessarily make the best decisions. Speed reading every sentence is slower than reading carefully and choosing once.
  4. Every choice has consequences. There is no "correct" path. Each option opens a different consequence that reveals what that choice reveals about your reasoning.
  5. Export your record at the end. You will receive a Word document with your three choices, the consequences, and reflection prompts to use as preparation for Assessment 3. Save it to your Assessment 3 working folder.
  6. Run it more than once. Different paths surface different reasoning. The most useful learning often comes on the second or third run.
Before you begin. Have your three drafted claims from Tutorial 11.3 open beside you — segment, scale, and key risk. The practice scenario will not test your specific concept, but the disciplines you bring from your own claims will carry across.

Decision Point 1 of 3  ·  The Segment Claim

The Head of Buying at the Major Grocer Has Read Your Brief

Investment Committee meeting — 14:32  ·  Boardroom B

You have presented the segment claim that anchors your business case. The Head of Buying from the major grocery retailer, who sits as an external observer on the Investment Committee, has been quiet through the first ten minutes. He now raises his hand.

Head of Buying — verbatim

"You have defined your target segment as 720,000 households — the multi-person homes who have switched to concentrated detergent in the last three years. That is a sharp definition. I respect it. But it is also the reason I cannot give you national shelf space. We do not list products that address less than two million households. So either your segment definition is wrong — and you are leaving households on the table — or your scale ambition is wrong, because a 720,000-household segment cannot support a national grocery launch. Tell me which it is. Because both cannot stand."

Your decision — 90 seconds
The Head of Buying expects a clear answer. The committee is watching. What do you say?
Consequence  ·  Decision 1

A Channel-First Defence

What happens next

You hold the segment. The Head of Buying nods slowly. The CFO writes something down. Two committee members look at each other. The chair speaks: "So you are telling us the segment claim is correct and the original channel plan was wrong. That is a meaningful concession. Walk us through the specialty-first economics — because if the unit economics do not work without the grocery channel, you have just removed your scale claim by accident."

You have protected one claim by exposing another. The committee will now press on the scale claim — harder than they would have if you had not made this trade. This is the cost of analytical honesty inside a room that wants a clean story.

Consequence  ·  Decision 1

A Wider Segment, A Weaker Story

What happens next

The Head of Buying is satisfied. The chair is satisfied. The Marketing Director, who has been quiet, leans forward: "Just to be clear — your original segment was the multi-person household experiencing a specific storage tension. The 1.4 million single-person buyers do not have that tension. They live alone. They have storage. So what is the tension that the wider segment is paying a premium to resolve? Because the premium pricing in your business case assumed the storage tension was doing the work."

You have just bought scale at the cost of segment specificity. The premium pricing claim that anchored your scale claim is no longer connected to a real tension for half your new segment. Decision 2 is going to be uncomfortable.

Consequence  ·  Decision 1

A Four-Week Pause

What happens next

The committee pauses. The chair confers briefly with the CFO. "We can grant four weeks. But we cannot grant four weeks of paralysis. So while you are running the deep dive, we want you to come back in two weeks with a working hypothesis about the adjacent segment, and in four weeks with the evidence to confirm or reject it. If you cannot confirm by week four, we close this round of funding."

You have bought analytical time at the cost of commercial momentum. The launch window has tightened. The committee will now ask the scale question with even less patience — they want to see that you are not delaying every decision to the same standard.

Time elapsed  ·  Decision 1

The Committee Filled the Silence

What happens next

Ninety seconds passed. You did not speak. The Head of Buying glanced at the chair. The chair filled the silence: "Let us return to that question after the next agenda item." It is the most polite way of saying the segment claim has just been registered as something you cannot defend in the room. The committee will return to it — but with less weight on your answer when you do give one.

In Assessment 3, a timeout is not a neutral outcome. The scenario records that you did not commit, and the next decision arrives with the previous question still open. This is what under-preparation feels like in an investment committee.

Decision Point 2 of 3  ·  The Scale Claim

The CFO Has Run the Numbers Independently

Investment Committee meeting — 14:48  ·  Boardroom B

The CFO has been listening. She has a Euromonitor printout in her hand. She slides it across the table.

CFO — verbatim

"Your scale claim assumes a 2 per cent share of the reachable segment in 18 months. The category is growing at 4 per cent annually — this is the published Euromonitor number, not yours. Here is what is on my mind. Category growth at 4 per cent absorbs roughly 1.2 per cent of the share you are claiming. The remaining 0.8 per cent has to come from incumbents. The incumbents in this category are Unilever and Procter and Gamble. So what you are actually telling this committee is that we are going to take 0.8 per cent of incumbent share from two of the largest consumer goods companies on earth, in 18 months, with our first product. I want to know: what makes you believe that?"

Notice what the CFO is doing. She is not contesting your evidence. She is testing whether your claim is internally consistent with the category economics. A share claim that requires an unrealistic incumbent-share take is not a scale claim — it is a hope.
Your decision — 90 seconds
Three viable answers. Each one tells the committee something different about how you read evidence.
Consequence  ·  Decision 2

Differentiation Versus Evidence

What happens next

The CFO does not push back immediately. She notes the differentiation argument and reserves judgement. The chair speaks: "We will accept the 2 per cent claim conditionally, contingent on the differentiation holding up under consumer testing in the first six months. If the test data does not show a structural difference, we will revisit the share claim before Year 2 budget is committed." Your scale claim survives intact but the burden of proof has shifted forward into the post-launch period.

Consequence  ·  Decision 2

A Smaller, Honester Number

What happens next

The CFO appreciates the concession. The chair does not. "If the revised number does not recover the tooling investment inside 24 months, you have not improved the business case — you have replaced an aspirational case with a non-viable case. Either find tooling savings, push payback to 30 months and tell us why that is acceptable, or restore some version of the incumbent-share argument with better evidence." You have protected analytical honesty at the cost of the investment thesis. Decision 3 arrives with the case wobbling.

Consequence  ·  Decision 2

A Sharper Denominator

What happens next

The CFO leans back. "That is a sharper claim than what you started with. But you have just made the subsegment carry the entire investment case. Tell me what happens if the subsegment turns out to be 30 per cent smaller than you have estimated — because the published data on subsegment-level willingness to pay is thinner than the category-level data. I am not arguing the subsegment is wrong. I am arguing the confidence interval around it is wider than you are showing." The committee accepts the reframe but asks for sensitivity analysis before sign-off.

Time elapsed  ·  Decision 2

A Number Without a Defence

What happens next

The 90 seconds elapsed in silence. The CFO leaves the Euromonitor printout on the table and turns to the chair. "We can come back to it." The committee moves on. Your scale claim is now in the room as an unanswered question. Decision 3 arrives with two open questions sitting behind it. This is what compounding under-preparation looks like.

Decision Point 3 of 3  ·  The Key Risk Claim

The General Counsel Has Just Joined

Investment Committee meeting — 15:04  ·  Boardroom B

The General Counsel was not on the original agenda. The chair invited her in fifteen minutes ago after the segment and scale discussion ran long. She has read your risk register on the way down. She speaks before she sits.

General Counsel — verbatim

"I will be direct. Your business case names the strongest marketing claim — the differentiated sustainability positioning — as the source of your premium price. Your risk register names that same claim as exposed to ACCC enforcement because the comparative evidence is not yet complete. So you have written a business case that depends, for its return, on a claim you cannot currently defend, which you have also told this committee about in writing. I respect the transparency. But I need to know what you propose we do about it — today — because the moment this paper leaves this room, the disclosed risk becomes a matter of record. Walk me through it."

What is at stake

4 months

This is the hardest decision in the scenario. The launch window is four months. The evidence to fully defend the claim takes six months. You cannot have both. The General Counsel is asking you to choose between commercial momentum, commercial integrity, and commercial timing — and she will hold you to whatever you say.
Your decision — 90 seconds
Three paths. Each one accepts a different cost. None of them is free.
Consequence  ·  Decision 3

Defensible But Second

What happens next

The General Counsel agrees. The CFO is uneasy — she has not seen the financial cost of the delay modelled. The chair calls the decision: launch is delayed by two months, the audit completes in time to support the lead claim at launch. The committee approves the development budget conditionally on the delayed timing. The competitor reaches market first, takes approximately 22 per cent of the early adopter segment, and is forced to soften its own claims in month three when its evidence base is challenged. You arrive second but with a defensible position. Whether this was the right trade depends on how recoverable second-mover share is in your category — and the data on that is not settled.

Consequence  ·  Decision 3

A Softer Claim, A Cleaner Launch

What happens next

The General Counsel co-signs. The Marketing Director objects briefly — the softened claim does not anchor a campaign as effectively as the original would have. The CFO models the impact: the lower premium pushes payback to 28 months from 24. The committee approves the case at the revised premium. Launch proceeds on schedule. The competitor reaches market two weeks after you with a stronger claim; you spend the first six months in a competitive defensibility argument you would not have had to fight at the original claim level. The decision was honest. The cost of the honesty is now visible.

Consequence  ·  Decision 3

A Window of Exposure

What happens next

The General Counsel asks for her objection to be minuted. The chair calls the decision: launch proceeds with the original claim, audit runs in parallel. Three weeks after launch, an industry blog runs an article questioning the claim. Four weeks after launch, the ACCC opens an informal inquiry. The brand pulls the lead claim voluntarily in week six and replaces it with a softened version — eight weeks earlier than required by enforcement. The premium has now eroded faster than Option B would have produced, and the brand reputation has absorbed the cost of the inquiry. The audit, when it completes, supports a stronger version of the original claim. By that point the launch has already had to absorb the climbdown. This is the most expensive of the three paths.

Time elapsed  ·  Decision 3

The Committee Decided Without You

What happens next

Ninety seconds elapsed. You had no answer. The General Counsel turned to the chair: "Without a clear position from the proposer, I cannot co-sign the risk register as it stands. I recommend the committee defer." The chair defers the decision. Your development budget is held pending a return paper. Two weeks later you bring the paper back — the decision is taken in a smaller room, by people who were not in the original meeting, with less context. The outcome is reasonable. The cost is that the decision was no longer yours to shape.

Three things to reflect on before Assessment 3
  1. Which decision did you find hardest? The hardest decision is usually the one that surfaces the weakest of your three claims in your own thinking. Open your Tutorial 11.3 draft and ask: is this claim weak because the evidence is weak, or because I have not yet done the translation move on it? The honest answer tells you where to spend your sharpening time.
  2. Where did you feel the time pressure most? The 90-second timer compresses what would normally be a 10-minute deliberation. In the assessed scenario, the timer is the same. Notice whether you defaulted to the option you understood fastest, or the option you believed in most. These are not always the same option, and the difference is diagnostic.
  3. What pattern did your three choices form? Were you mostly defending your original claims (A patterns), mostly revising under pressure (B patterns), or mostly reframing the question (C patterns)? None of these is wrong. The pattern is information about how you read evidence under commercial pressure — useful information to bring into Assessment 3.
